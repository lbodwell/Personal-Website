Relatable DAW is an online, collaborative, interactive digital audio workstation developed with the MERN stack, originally created as part of an IQP at WPI as a solo effort. Its goal is to teach music theory concepts through relation-based composition. Audio synthesis is handled with Tone.js, and the combination of a NoSQL database and web sockets allow users to save, share, and collaborate simultaneously on projects after signing in via Google OAuth. This project was developed and iterated on based on the insights of a professional high school band director.

GeoBlade is a 3D third-person action-adventure game in which audio and music play a major design role. It is developed using Unity and was originally created as part of an MQP at WPI with two other students. I focused mostly on gameplay programming, systems programming, audio, music, and writing. The game features a rich, location-driven narrative complete with original music, fully voice-acted dialogue, and dynamic sound effects implemented in Audiokinetic Wwise. Players will face challenges in the form of both melee combat and puzzles as they meet friends, foes, and those in between while unravelling the truth of this mysterious world.

This application was developed as part of a software engineering class at WPI with a team of seven other students. I served as the assistant lead developer and database engineer of my team, assigning tasks to others and overseeing the database, user authentication, and notifications systems. > Sticker/Chat App

This is a native Android application that was developed as part of a mobile/ubiquitous computing class at WPI with two other students. I worked primarily on the backend of the application but also assisted with hooking things up on the frontend. Firebase was used to handle both authentication and realtime data storage. The app allows users to log in using either a Google account or an email and password, then choose from a series of event-themed stickers to send to a friend. A personalized message and an accompanying photo taken with the device's camera can be sent alongside the sticker, and the two users can then follow up on the exchange with further realtime chat.

> Mobile Renderer

JavaScript

WebGL

Graphics

Rendering

Shaders

Lighting

This project was created as part of a computer graphics class at WPI. It uses WebGL, vanilla JavaScript, and custom vertex/fragment shaders to hierarchically render a spinning mobile comprised of cubes and spheres of various materials, each with unique ambient, specular, and diffuse lighting behavior. The renderer implements toggleable shadows, textures, and reflection and refraction using a cubemap, as well as both flat and Gouroud shading. The mobile is lit using a point light with an adjustable position and cone angle, and the perspective of the camera can be moved to any position within the confines of the scene. Please note that the sphere model file must be uploaded to the demo site manually. It can be downloaded here.
